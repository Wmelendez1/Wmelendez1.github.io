Month Two Progress Update

Author: Walter Melendez

Date: July 22, 2026

Month Two Overview

The second month of my Master's Certificate in Data Science capstone focused on transforming my completed machine learning solution into a polished research project and preparing for my final defense. During this phase, I shifted from development to validation, documentation, and presentation.

One of the most significant milestones this month was evaluating multiple machine learning models to determine which would best support admissions lead prioritization. I trained and compared Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Support Vector Machine models using a public higher education lead conversion dataset.

Each model was evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, confusion matrices, and 10-fold cross-validation. Using cross-validation helped provide a more reliable evaluation than relying on a single training and testing split.

After comparing the models, Random Forest demonstrated the strongest overall predictive performance and was selected as the final model. The model also generated conversion probability scores that allowed leads to be organized into High, Medium, and Low priority groups.

Dashboard and Presentation Development

Another major accomplishment was completing the Tableau dashboards used to visualize the project results. The dashboards summarize key performance indicators, model predictions, lead priority groups, probability distributions, and model comparisons.

I also continued refining my IEEE-formatted thesis and defense presentation. Feedback from my advisor helped me improve the scientific explanation of the project, clarify the machine learning workflow, and make the presentation easier for the audience to follow.

Challenges and Lessons Learned

One of the most challenging parts of this month was learning how to present the project as scientific research rather than simply showing the final model results. I needed to clearly explain how the data was prepared, why certain variables were selected, how the models were evaluated, and what the results meant for the business problem.

This experience reinforced that a successful machine learning project requires more than producing an accurate model. The process must also be reproducible, supported by research, evaluated using appropriate methods, and communicated clearly to both technical and non-technical audiences.

Looking Ahead

My remaining work includes making final revisions to the thesis and presentation, continuing to practice my defense, reviewing potential committee questions, and completing a final quality assurance review of all project deliverables.

Overall, Month Two represented the transition from building a machine learning solution to presenting it as a complete research project supported by scientific methodology, statistical evaluation, and practical business applications.
